Mpox (Monkeypox) — Quick Facts & Guidance

Reliable, concise information about mpox: symptoms, transmission, prevention, and treatment.

What is mpox?

Mpox (formerly called monkeypox) is an infection caused by the monkeypox virus (a member of the orthopoxvirus family). It can cause fever, swollen lymph nodes and a characteristic rash that progresses through several stages before healing. While most people recover with supportive care, some cases can be more severe.

Common symptoms

  • Fever, headache, muscle aches, and fatigue.
  • Swollen lymph nodes (a distinguishing early sign).
  • A rash that begins as flat patches → raised bumps → fluid-filled blisters → pustules → scabs. Lesions may be localized or widespread.
  • People are typically infectious from symptom onset until all lesions have crusted and healed.

How it spreads

Mpox spreads through close, sustained skin-to-skin contact with lesions, body fluids, or contaminated materials (bedding, clothing). It can also spread via respiratory droplets during prolonged face-to-face contact. Recent outbreaks have shown transmission during intimate/sexual contact in certain networks.

Prevention

  • Avoid close skin-to-skin contact with people who have a rash that looks like mpox.
  • Do not share bedding, towels, clothing, or utensils with someone who is infected.
  • Practice safer sex and reduce number of close contacts during outbreaks.
  • Vaccination is available for people at higher risk and may be recommended in some settings — follow local public health guidance.
If you think you were exposed or have symptoms, contact your healthcare provider or local health department for testing and isolation guidance.

Treatment & care

There is no widely approved antiviral specifically for mpox for all patients; most people receive supportive care (pain control, wound care, hydration, and treating secondary infections). Antivirals such as tecovirimat (where available) may be used in severe cases or for people at high risk, under clinician guidance.

When to seek medical care

  • If you develop a new unexplained rash or lesions, especially with fever or swollen lymph nodes.
  • If you have had close contact with someone diagnosed with mpox.
  • If you are immunocompromised, pregnant, very young, or have severe symptoms — seek care promptly.

Practical tips for households

  • Isolate the person with mpox from others until all lesions have healed and scabs have fallen off.
  • Use gloves when touching lesions or contaminated materials; wash hands frequently.
  • Launder bedding and clothes with hot water and detergent; avoid shaking items to prevent dispersal of infectious material.
Fast facts

Also called: Monkeypox (common) / Mpox (preferred term by some health agencies)

Cause: Monkeypox virus (an orthopoxvirus)

Incubation: ~5–21 days (commonly 6–13 days)

Infectious period: From symptom onset until lesions have healed)

If exposed

Contact local public health for testing, vaccination recommendations, and advice about quarantine or monitoring.
